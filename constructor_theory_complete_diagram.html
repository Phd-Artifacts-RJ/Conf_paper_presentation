1Ô∏è‚É£ CONSTRAINTS (Laws of Physics)

These are the fundamental rules that determine which tasks are possible and which are impossible.

Example: Conservation of Mass
Constraint: Atoms cannot be created or destroyed in chemical reactions

Application: You can rearrange amino acids into a protein chain, but you cannot create or lose atoms. Therefore, the mass of products must equal the mass of reactants (plus any cofactors).

This constraint makes the protein synthesis task possible (atoms can be rearranged) but forbids tasks that would create atoms from nothing.
Example: Second Law of Thermodynamics
Constraint: Entropy cannot spontaneously decrease in an isolated system

Application: Protein synthesis is only possible if you input energy (ATP). The task of building complex ordered proteins requires energy input to overcome entropy. The reverse task (decomposing proteins back to amino acids) could happen spontaneously, making it more thermodynamically favorable.

üîë KEY INSIGHTS FROM THIS EXAMPLE

1. Information is Physical
The mRNA sequence is not abstract information‚Äîit's a physical molecule that can be measured, copied, and transported. Information exists in the physical differences between possible states (different codon sequences are distinguishable physical states). This is why Constructor Theory can treat information as governed by physical laws.
2. Constructor Universality Limits
The ribosome is a programmable constructor for protein synthesis, but NOT a universal constructor. It can only perform tasks related to translating mRNA to protein. A universal constructor would be able to perform ANY physically possible task when given the right program. Whether universal constructors can exist is an open question in Constructor Theory.
3. Irreversibility from Task Asymmetry
Notice we didn't need to mention time or entropy explicitly! The fact that T ‚úì but T~ ‚úò automatically encodes that protein synthesis is a time-directed process. Constructor Theory naturally explains irreversibility through the existence of impossible tasks, rather than through statistical mechanics arguments about disorder.
4. Knowledge as Abstract Constructor
The "knowledge" of how to build a specific protein is instantiated in the DNA sequence. This knowledge survives because mRNA keeps transcribing it, and tRNA keeps reading it accurately. Knowledge here is defined (following Popper) as "information that tends to remain instantiated despite noise and errors." This is abstract‚Äîbut it's still a physical phenomenon.
5. Substrate-Independence
The biological information in the genetic code could, in principle, be instantiated in any physical medium: DNA, RNA, synthetic DNA, even digital sequences written on paper. The information itself is substrate-independent‚Äîwhat matters is the pattern, not the physical medium. This is why information is a fundamental concept in Constructor Theory.

‚ú® Summary

Constructor Theory provides a unified language for understanding physical processes by asking a simple question: Which transformations are possible, and which are impossible, and why?

In the protein synthesis example, Constructor Theory shows us that:

  • The task (making protein) is defined purely by input-output specification
  • The constructor (ribosome) performs it reliably
  • The program (mRNA) is physical information that guides the constructor
  • The substrates (amino acids) undergo transformation
  • The constraints (physical laws) determine what's possible
  • Task composition allows building complex processes from simple ones
  • Task transposition reveals irreversibility and time direction
This same framework applies to chemistry (catalysis), thermodynamics (heat engines), computation (universal computers), quantum mechanics (quantum information), and potentially to life and evolution itself. That's the power and elegance of Constructor Theory.
